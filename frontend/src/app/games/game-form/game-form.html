What is complexity?

Complexity (BGG's "weight") rates how mentally demanding a game is, on a scale of 1–5:

  • 1 — Very Light: Simple rules, minimal decisions. Great for kids or non-gamers.
  • 2 — Light: Easy to learn, a few meaningful choices. Good gateway games. (e.g. Ticket to Ride, Catan)
  • 3 — Medium: Moderate ruleset, real strategic depth. (e.g. Pandemic, 7 Wonders)
  • 4 — Heavy: Complex rules, long play time, deep strategy. (e.g. Terraforming Mars)
  • 5 — Very Heavy: Steep learning curve, highly involved. (e.g. Twilight Imperium)

Use the suggestion page to filter by complexity range — handy when you want a light game for a casual night or a brain-burner for experienced players.

How to find mechanics

Ask what players spend most of their time doing:

  • Placing pieces on a board → Tile Placement
  • Drawing and playing cards from a hand → Hand Management
  • Rolling dice to determine outcomes → Dice Rolling
  • Building up a personal card supply → Deck Building
  • Sending workers to claim actions → Worker Placement
  • Collecting matching sets → Set Collection
  • Competing to control board regions → Area Control

Look at what's in the box:

  • Lots of dice → Dice Rolling or Push Your Luck
  • A big deck of cards → Deck Building or Hand Management
  • Worker meeples → Worker Placement
  • A map with regions → Area Control
  • Hidden role cards → Hidden Roles or Bluffing

Category = the theme (what it's about). Mechanic = the verb (what you do). Most games have 3–6 mechanics. Describe your turn in plain English — each verb is usually a mechanic.

How to pick categories

Theme — what the game is about:

  • Castles & knights → Medieval
  • Spaceships & aliens → Science Fiction / Space
  • Dragons & magic → Fantasy
  • Battles & armies → Wargame / World War II
  • Murder mystery → Deduction

Mechanism — how you play:

  • Building a personal card deck → Deck Building
  • Everyone works together → Cooperative
  • One player lies, others guess → Bluffing / Social Deduction
  • Placing tiles on a board → Tile Game
  • Mainly rolling dice → Dice Game

Audience — who it's for:

  • Family — accessible to all ages, low complexity
  • Party — 5+ players, fast and social
  • Abstract — no theme at all (Chess, Go, Othello)

Most games have 2–3 categories. Ask yourself: what's the box art about? What do players spend most of their time doing?
